## What This Document Is

This user guide walks you through SA Esports Hub from your very first visit through every major feature the platform offers. It's written for people who have never used the system before and assumes no technical background. By following this guide step by step, you'll experience the complete platform in about 15 to 20 minutes and understand both what it does and why it matters for South African esports.

The guide covers everything from browsing as a guest visitor through creating your account, building your competitive profile, joining tournaments, scouting other players, managing your settings, and exploring the administrative panel. Along the way, it points out the design touches and animations that make the platform feel premium and polished.

## Getting Started

When you first visit SA Esports Hub at  https://sa-esport-hub.onrender.com , you'll arrive at the home page which serves as your entry point to everything the platform offers. The page greets you with a striking hero section featuring an animated graphic and the message "South Africa's Esports Hub" prominently displayed. Below the hero you'll see a stats bar showing how many leagues are active, how many players are ranked, how many provinces are represented, and how many games are supported.

Scrolling down reveals featured leagues showing the latest tournaments that gamers can join, followed by the top ranked players section displaying the best competitors in the country with gold, silver, and bronze trophy icons for the top three positions. Further down you'll find six feature cards explaining what the platform does — league management, national rankings, scout reports, province coverage, looking for team features, and verified profiles. If you're not logged in, a call-to-action section at the bottom invites you to join and start competing.

The entire page sits on top of an animated background featuring a shifting mesh gradient, a drifting dot grid, three floating green orbs, a network of 80 connected particles that react to your mouse movement, and a green scan line that periodically sweeps from top to bottom. Take a moment to move your mouse around the page and watch the particles repel from your cursor. This animated background appears on every page throughout the entire platform.

## Browsing as a Guest

You don't need an account to explore most of SA Esports Hub. Many features are accessible to anyone visiting the site, which lets you evaluate the platform before deciding to register.

Click the Leagues link in the top navigation menu to see all tournaments currently happening or scheduled. Each league appears as a card showing the game it's for, its status (upcoming, active, completed, or cancelled), a brief description, the start date, how many participants have signed up out of the maximum allowed, and the prize pool if there is one. Click any league card to see its full details including who organized it, the complete description, the tournament format, the rules, the current participants, and any matches that have been played.

Click the Players link in the top navigation to see the national rankings. Players appear in a card layout sorted by their ranking points from highest to lowest. The top three positions display special trophy icons — gold for first place, silver for second, and bronze for third. Each player card shows their gamertag, primary game, skill tier, ranking points, win rate, and a button to view their full profile. Click any player to see their complete competitive profile including all their stats, tournament history, scouting reports written about them, and their highlight reel if they've added one.

What you can't do as a guest is anything that creates or modifies data. You can't register for leagues, you can't create a player profile, you can't submit scouting reports, and you can't edit anything. To do these things, you need to create an account.

## Creating Your Account

To get the full SA Esports Hub experience, you need to register. The process takes about 60 seconds and requires no email verification, making it quick and easy.

Click the Join button in the top right corner of the navigation. You'll be taken to the registration form which is presented in a glassmorphic card with a pulsing Xbox logo at the top. The form asks for several pieces of information. Your username is what you'll log in with — it must be unique across the platform. Your Xbox Gamertag is your competitive identity — this is what other players, scouts, and organizers will see when they look at your profile, so choose something memorable. Your email is required for account purposes. Your role determines what you primarily do on the platform — pick Player if you compete in tournaments, Scout if you evaluate talent, Organizer if you run tournaments, or Spectator if you just watch. Your province is one of South Africa's nine provinces. Your password must be at least eight characters and shouldn't be too common or too similar to your username.

When you click Create Account, the system validates everything you entered, creates your account, automatically logs you in, and redirects you to the home page with a welcome message. You'll notice your gamertag now appears in the top right corner of the navigation where the Join button used to be. Click your gamertag at any time to access your profile, your player stats if you've created them, and the sign out option.

## Building Your Player Profile

Having an account is just the start. To appear in the national rankings and be discoverable by scouts and teams, you need to create a Player Profile. This is separate from your basic account because not everyone who uses the platform is necessarily a competitive player — some are scouts, organizers, or just fans.

Click your gamertag in the top right and select Create Player Profile from the dropdown. You'll be taken to a form asking for your competitive details. Your primary game is the title you compete in most — pick from the six supported games. Your skill tier is your current competitive level ranging from Bronze at the bottom through Silver, Gold, Platinum, Diamond, Master, and Grand Master at the top. Be honest about your tier since misrepresenting yourself hurts your credibility.

Your ranking points are a numeric score representing your competitive standing — start with a number you feel reflects your actual skill (typical ranges are 500 for beginners, 1500 for intermediate, 2500+ for advanced players). Your wins, losses, and draws are your match record. Your tournament wins is how many competitions you've won outright. The available for scouting checkbox lets scouts evaluate you — keep it checked unless you specifically don't want feedback. The looking for team checkbox tells coaches and team managers that you're available to join their roster. The highlights URL is optional but powerful — link to a YouTube playlist or Twitch highlights showing your best gameplay.

After submitting the form, you're immediately listed in the national rankings. Click the Players link in the navigation and you should see yourself in the rankings table. Click your name to view your full profile page which is now publicly visible to everyone on the platform.

## Creating Your Own League

If you want to run a tournament, SA Esports Hub makes it dramatically easier than coordinating through Discord and spreadsheets. Click the Leagues link in the navigation and then click the Create League button in the top right.

The league creation form walks you through everything needed. The league name should be descriptive — something like "SA Tekken Championship 2024" or "Gauteng Street Fighter Open" rather than just "Tournament 1." Pick the game from the dropdown. Write a description explaining what the tournament is about, who it's for, and why people should join. Select the format from single elimination (one loss eliminates you), double elimination (you need two losses to be out), round robin (everyone plays everyone), Swiss system (paired against similarly-ranked opponents), or group stage plus playoffs (groups first, then knockouts).

The status defaults to upcoming which is correct for a new tournament. Set the maximum participants based on how many you can accommodate — common values are 16, 32, or 64 for elimination formats. The prize pool is in South African Rand and can be zero if you're running a casual tournament without money prizes. Set the start date for when the tournament begins and the end date for when you expect it to conclude. The registration deadline is when sign-ups close — set it a few days before the start date so you have time to finalize the bracket. You can optionally upload a banner image which makes your league look more professional in the listings. Finally write out the rules covering things like which characters or weapons are banned, how disputes are resolved, what happens if a player no-shows, and any other tournament-specific policies.

After clicking Create League, your tournament is immediately published and visible to all users on the platform. They can browse to it from the leagues list, view its details, and register to participate. As the organizer you have additional powers — you can edit any details, approve or reject registrations, record match results, and manage the entire tournament workflow.

## Registering for a League

To join a tournament organized by someone else, navigate to the Leagues page and browse the available leagues. Look for tournaments with the upcoming status and that have spots remaining (the participant count shows how many of the maximum slots are filled). Click any league that interests you to see its full details.

On the league detail page, you'll see comprehensive information including the description, format, dates, prize pool, current participants, and rules. If the league is open for registration and you're logged in, you'll see a Register button. Click it to submit your registration. You'll see a confirmation message saying you're registered but awaiting approval.

The approval workflow exists because organizers need to control who joins their tournaments. They might want to verify your skill level matches the tournament's intended bracket, ensure you've paid an entry fee if one exists, or simply confirm you're a legitimate participant. The organizer receives your registration and can approve, reject, or leave it pending. Once approved, you officially appear in the participants list and can be paired in matches.

You can register for as many tournaments as you want simultaneously. Your registration status across all leagues you've joined is visible from your profile area. If you change your mind, you can withdraw from a tournament before it starts, though this should be done sparingly as it disrupts the bracket for other players.

## Scouting Other Players

The scouting system lets experienced players, coaches, and talent evaluators provide structured feedback on competitive players. This builds a portfolio of evaluations that helps everyone — players get useful feedback to improve, teams identify quality recruits, and the community develops a shared understanding of who the rising stars are.

To scout a player, navigate to the Players page and click on any player who isn't yourself. On their profile page you'll see a Scout Report button in the top right. Click it to access the evaluation form.

The form asks you to rate four distinct aspects of the player's gameplay on a scale from 1 to 10. The overall rating reflects your holistic impression of their skill level. The mechanics rating evaluates their technical execution — button input speed, accuracy, character or weapon control, and precision under pressure. The game sense rating measures their strategic thinking, decision making, map awareness, and ability to read opponents. The consistency rating assesses whether they perform at the same level every match or only show their best occasionally.

Below the ratings you write notes explaining your evaluation. Good notes are specific and constructive — instead of "great player," write "strong rushdown game but defensive options need work, particularly punishing whiffed attacks." This actionable feedback is what makes scout reports valuable beyond just a numeric score.

The recommended checkbox indicates whether you'd actively suggest this player to a team or sponsor. Use it judiciously since it's a strong endorsement of their professional potential.

After submitting your report, it appears immediately on the player's profile page where everyone can see it. The player and any scouts visiting their profile can read your evaluation. Each scout can only write one report per player which keeps the system honest and prevents abuse. If you want to update a scout report you've written, you'd need to delete the old one (through the admin panel) and submit a new one.

## Managing Your Profile

As you use the platform, you'll want to update your profile to reflect your current information. Click your gamertag in the top right and select My Profile from the dropdown.

The profile page shows your current information at the top including your avatar, gamertag, role, and any bio you've written. Below this is the edit form divided into two sections. The Account Info section handles standard user details like your first name, last name, and email address. The Esports Profile section handles platform-specific details including your gamertag, role, province, avatar image, biography, and Xbox profile URL.

Click Choose File next to the avatar field to upload a profile picture. The platform accepts standard image formats and resizes them appropriately. A square image works best — circular cropping is applied automatically. Your bio can be up to 500 characters and is a good place to mention what games you play, when you stream, what teams you've been part of, or what your competitive goals are.

After making changes, click Save Changes to apply them. The page refreshes with your updates visible. Other users will see the new information immediately on your profile and anywhere your account appears such as in scout reports you've written or leagues you've organized.

If you have a player profile separately from your basic account, you can edit it from the My Stats link in the dropdown. This takes you to your player profile where you can update your stats, skill tier, scouting availability, and other competitive details.

## The Administrative Panel

Behind the scenes, SA Esports Hub has a sophisticated admin panel that platform managers use to oversee the entire system. While regular users don't see this side of the platform, understanding it helps you appreciate the work happening to keep things running smoothly. If you have administrative access, you can explore it directly.

Navigate to /admin/ in the URL bar and log in with admin credentials. Instead of Django's default admin interface, you'll see a completely custom dashboard. A live clock in the top right updates every second showing the current time. Four stat cards across the top display animated counters for active leagues, ranked players, registered users, and scouting reports. Each card animates from zero to its actual value when the page loads and serves as a clickable shortcut to manage that category.

The quick actions panel below the stat cards provides six one-click shortcuts for the most common administrative tasks. Create League opens the league creation form. Add Player opens the player profile creation form. New User adds a user manually without requiring them to register themselves. Log Match records a match result. Approve Signups jumps to the pending registrations list. Manage Profiles takes you to all user profiles. These shortcuts dramatically reduce the clicks needed for routine work.

The left sidebar contains a permanently visible navigation menu grouped into three sections. The Overview section has the dashboard and a link to view the live site in a new tab. The Esports Hub section contains leagues, registrations, matches, players, and scout reports. The Members section includes users, profiles, and groups. Click any section item to access the management interface for that data type.

The right side of the dashboard shows a real-time activity feed listing the latest 12 administrative actions across the entire platform. Each action is color-coded with green dots for additions, yellow dots for modifications, and red dots for deletions. The feed shows what was changed, the content type, and how long ago the action occurred. This gives administrators instant visibility into what's happening on the platform.

When you click into any data section like Players or Leagues, you get a table view of all records with filtering, searching, and sorting capabilities. Click any record to edit it. Use the filters on the right to narrow down the list by game, status, province, or other criteria. The search bar at the top lets you find specific records quickly. Bulk actions let you perform operations on multiple records at once.

## Putting It All Together

Now that you've seen the major features individually, here's a typical user journey that ties everything together. Imagine you're a competitive Tekken player from Durban who just heard about the platform.

You start by visiting the site and browsing the home page to see what it offers. The animated background and professional design make a good first impression. You click Players and browse the rankings, getting a sense of the competitive scene. You see several Tekken players ranked nationally and recognize a few names from local tournaments. You click their profiles to see their stats and read scouting reports about them.

Convinced this is worth joining, you click the Join button and register your account. You pick "Player" as your role and choose KwaZulu-Natal as your province. You create your gamertag as TekkenKing_DBN. After registering, you immediately create your player profile listing Tekken 8 as your primary game with a skill tier of Platinum and 65 wins against 28 losses.

You navigate to the Leagues section and find an upcoming national Tekken 8 championship with a R5,000 prize pool. You click Register and wait for the organizer's approval. Meanwhile you click on another player you've competed against before, an Master-tier player from Gauteng, and submit a scouting report giving them high marks for mechanics and game sense.

You go back to your profile and upload an avatar image, write a bio mentioning your goals, and add a YouTube link to your highlight reel. You sign out feeling like you've established a real competitive presence on a professional platform.

The next day you sign back in and see that the tournament organizer approved your registration. You're now officially in the championship bracket. You watch other players you registered with also get approved. Over the following weeks, the organizer records match results as the tournament progresses. Each result updates win/loss records and reshapes the national rankings.

This journey — discover, register, compete, get scouted, climb the rankings — is exactly what the platform was built to enable. By following this guide you've experienced all the major touch points that make this journey possible.

## Design Highlights Worth Noticing

While you're using SA Esports Hub, take a moment to appreciate the polish and craftsmanship in the design. These details might seem small individually but together they create a premium experience that elevates the platform above typical web applications.

The animated background runs continuously behind every page combining five layered effects — a shifting mesh gradient, a subtle drifting grid, three floating glow orbs, a network of connected particles that respond to your mouse, and a green scan line that periodically sweeps down the screen. Move your mouse around any page and watch how the particles repel from your cursor like a force field.

The typography uses Rajdhani for headings which has a distinctly gaming-inspired character with its sharp geometric forms, while Inter handles body text for excellent readability. The combination feels both professional and authentically gaming.

All cards, panels, and the navigation bar use glassmorphism — a frosted glass effect created with semi-transparent backgrounds and backdrop blur. This lets the animated background show through subtly, giving the interface depth and layering rather than feeling flat.

Interactive elements have layered animations that respond to user actions. Buttons display a ripple effect when clicked, growing outward from where you clicked. They scale up slightly on hover and gain a glowing shadow. Cards lift up on hover with a green border highlight. Stat numbers animate from zero to their target value when you scroll them into view. Player cards have a green accent bar that slides in from the left when you hover over them.

The Xbox logo in the navigation pulses with a green glow that intensifies and fades. The top three players in rankings display gold, silver, and bronze trophy icons with their colors and a soft glow. Active league status badges pulse with a subtle animation indicating they're live. The admin dashboard's live clock updates every second so you always see the current time.

These touches together create what designers call a "delightful experience" — interactions that not only function but actively feel good to use. The platform deliberately uses the same design language across both the user-facing site and the administrative panel so administrators don't feel like they've stepped into a different application when they need to manage things.

## Common Questions

People new to the platform often have similar questions. Here are answers to the most common ones.

You don't need an actual Xbox console to use the platform. The "Xbox Gamertag" field is simply a username — it's called that because Xbox is the platform's branding inspiration, but you can use any name you want as your competitive identity. The platform works for PC players, PlayStation players, and Xbox players equally well.

If you want to delete your account, contact an administrator who can remove it through the admin panel. The system doesn't currently include a self-service account deletion option since it requires careful handling to maintain data integrity — for example, if you've written scout reports about other players, those reports need handling appropriately when your account is removed.

The reason you have to wait for league approval is that organizers control who joins their tournaments. This prevents spam registrations, lets organizers verify eligibility for skill-restricted brackets, and gives them a chance to confirm everyone is legitimate. For most casual tournaments, approval happens quickly. For high-stakes events with prize money, organizers may take longer to verify each participant.

You can't scout yourself because the system specifically prevents it. Self-evaluation would obviously be biased and would compromise the integrity of the scouting system. If you want self-reflection on your gameplay, ask another scout you trust to evaluate you.

The one-report-per-player limit on scouting exists for similar reasons. Allowing scouts to spam multiple reports on the same player would let them artificially inflate or deflate that player's standing. The limit ensures each scout's perspective is represented once, keeping reports trustworthy.

Rankings are currently calculated based on the ranking points you set in your player profile. In future updates, this will become more sophisticated by automatically calculating points based on match results, tournament wins, and the strength of opponents defeated. For now, the points system relies on honest self-reporting backed by the visible win/loss record.

All profiles are public by design. This is because the platform's purpose is to make competitive players discoverable. If you don't want to be discoverable, you can uncheck the available for scouting flag on your player profile, which signals to scouts and teams that you're not currently looking for opportunities.

Your account data is reasonably secure given this is a demonstration platform. Passwords are hashed using Django's secure PBKDF2 algorithm so they can't be read even by administrators. The site uses HTTPS to encrypt your traffic. However, since this is a demo platform with publicly documented credentials, don't enter sensitive personal information beyond what's needed for testing, and don't use passwords from other accounts you care about.
